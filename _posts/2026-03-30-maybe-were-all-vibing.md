After all that AI and LLM extravaganza, I decided to step back from that topic for now. I decided to focus on other projects that are better aligned to what I actually need in my day to day life, and to what will benefit the community. For now, I have taken on a new biomedical related project but I am not yet revealing what it is here in this post. I feel like it's barely just a version 1 of what I actually want to make and you might all laugh at me lol. Anyway, my approach to this project is to minimize the help of AI generation and just do my own thing really, and write the code the way I want it to. But that got me thinking deeply. "Is what I am doing now actually different from vibe coding?"

I still use AI when I build, although instead of asking for the whole code directly, I just ask it what libraries I should be using, or how do I build this function, or what does this concept mean, or if it can check my work for any errors that I might have overlooked. And I know what you're thinking. "Isn't that just vibe coding with extra steps?"

And to be honest with you, I don't really think that it is, but the line is thinner than I had assumed.

The way that I look at it, it's not just about whether or not we use AI. Rather, it's about what you do with what it gives you. When I first started coding CH3SH1RE, I was just accepting output. I was accepting a blob and approving code that I hadn't read or understood. I was running functions that I couldn't explain even in the simplest terms and I was patching errors by just generating replacements. At that time, the AI felt more like the author and I was just the yes man who passes everything.

But now it's different. And I want you to really hear me out on this because my whole point might sound controversial. When I ask an AI to give me a list of possible libraries to use or to show me a function structure, isn't that just the same as asking other developers on Stack Overflow, or looking at code snippets on a random GitHub repo, or reading a library's documentation manually? I'm not asking AI for the whole code right away but rather asking it to fetch information like I would ask on a subreddit. I go in with a specific question. I get a specific answer. I take that answer and do something with it myself, whether I test it or break it on purpose, it doesn't really matter.

And maybe that's the only real measure of whether you're actually learning. Not where the information came from, but what you can do with it once the tab is closed. If I can close the conversation and still write the function, then cool, something stuck. If I can't, then I was just vibe coding in slow motion. And look, you might argue that I'm being inefficient, that by now I could have had a fully working app already. And yeah, fair enough. But it doesn't bother me. I'd rather be slow and actually get something out of it than be fast and end up staring at code I don't recognize again.

And honestly, CH3SH1RE had to happen first. The vibe coded version, the hollow confidence, the moment of looking at my own code and not recognizing it. That was the education. Everything after that is just me actually showing up to class.
